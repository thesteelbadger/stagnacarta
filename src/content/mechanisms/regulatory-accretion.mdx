## How It Works

A problem occurs. A regulation is created to prevent it. The regulation works — or at least, the problem doesn't recur in exactly the same form. The regulation stays. Another problem occurs. Another regulation. The process is one-directional because the political cost of repealing any individual regulation always exceeds the political cost of keeping it. If a future government repeals Part O and someone subsequently falls from a window, that death will be attributed to the repeal.

Each regulation is individually defensible. Nobody argues for children falling from windows, or bats going extinct, or buildings catching fire. But the aggregate weight — the cumulative cost of complying with every regulation simultaneously — is never owned by any institution or the government of the day.

## Where It Appears

**Part O window regulations**: Non-ground floor windows must be 1.1m from the floor to prevent falls. Falls from windows account for 0.8% of domestic accidents, based on data over 20 years old. The consequence: darker homes, near-impossible to match existing architectural styles, effective ban on upstairs sash windows. Nobody will repeal this because the headline risk ("Government removes safety protection from windows") always outweighs the invisible cost (millions of uglier, darker homes).

**Bechstein's bat and HS2**: A conservation survey discovered bats where none were previously recorded. The regulatory machinery automatically triggered planning restrictions across thousands of square kilometres. No minister signed it off. No democratic process authorised it. Result: a £100 million bat tunnel that won't be used by the bats.

**Environmental assessments**: The West Midlands Metro one-mile extension required 3,201 pages of environmental assessment for laying tracks on existing urban roads. The assessment is proportionate to the worst-case scenario of the most complex project, applied uniformly to the simplest.

**British tram costs**: British trams cost roughly double the European average to build. Utility diversion regulations (SI 2000/3314) shifted costs 92.5/7.5 against tram projects — a statutory instrument with no parliamentary debate, no public consultation, and no published rationale. It has been in force for 26 years.

## Why It Only Goes One Way

The asymmetry is structural. The beneficiaries of each individual regulation are concentrated and identifiable (bat conservationists, window safety campaigners, utility companies). The costs are diffuse and invisible (everyone who lives in a darker home, every commuter on a bus that should be a tram, every taxpayer funding infrastructure at double the European cost).

Concentrated benefits and diffuse costs is the textbook formula for regulatory persistence. Add scar tissue — the Edinburgh tram disaster, Grenfell — and each failure generates new regulation that makes the next project harder, which increases the probability of the next failure, which generates more regulation.

## The Comparison

France builds tram systems routinely. French environmental assessments are proportionate. French utility companies pay their own diversion costs. None of this requires French engineering genius. It requires not actively obstructing yourself.

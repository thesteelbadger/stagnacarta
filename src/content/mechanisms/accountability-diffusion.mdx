## How It Works

A problem falls across the boundaries of multiple institutions. Each institution addresses the part that falls within its mandate and ignores the rest. The aggregate outcome — which is what the public actually experiences — is owned by nobody.

This is not buck-passing in the individual sense. Each institution is doing its job. The Environment Agency protects the environment. Local authorities represent local residents. The DfE sets education policy. The NHS provides assessments. None of them is wrong within their own frame. The systemic cost of their collective action falls on nobody in particular.

## Where It Appears

**SEND**: The DfE sets the policy. Local authorities fund it. The NHS assesses it. Tribunals enforce it. Nobody owns the gap between statutory entitlement and deliverable reality. Parents bear the cost.

**HS2 Wendover**: The Environment Agency mandates a spring chamber. HS2 designs minimum intervention. Buckinghamshire Council blocks it on landscape grounds. Each organ optimises for its own metric. The tens of millions in delay costs fall on the national taxpayer, who has no representative in the room.

**NS&I bereavement scandal**: NS&I lost track of investments. Atos (outsourced operator) managed the process. The Treasury knew. 37,500 families over 18 years fell through the gap between three organisations, each pointing at the others.

**Al-Quds Day**: The Met says it's for the CPS. CPS says insufficient evidence. Home Secretary bans the march but allows the static protest. Charity Commission has never deregistered the organising body. Ofcom investigated the broadcast. Everyone has investigated. Nobody has acted.

**ISC Russia Report**: Treasury optimised for investment flows. MI5 optimised for counter-terrorism. DCMS thought disinformation was someone else's problem. Electoral Commission lacked tools. Nobody owned "protecting democratic processes from hostile state interference." The ISC called it a "hot potato."

## The Structural Feature

Accountability diffusion is not a bug in British governance — it is the architecture. The post-war settlement deliberately distributed power to prevent concentration. The unintended consequence is that systemic problems — problems that exist precisely at the boundaries between institutions — have no owner.

The correction would require someone with authority across institutional boundaries. That person is theoretically the Prime Minister or the Cabinet. In practice, the PM's bandwidth is consumed by the Wub Problem — each administration is overwhelmed by existing institutional dynamics before it can address structural ones.

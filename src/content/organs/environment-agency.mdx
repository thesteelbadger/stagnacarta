## What It Does

The Environment Agency is responsible for environmental protection, flood risk management, water quality, fisheries, navigation, and waste regulation in England. It employs approximately 10,600 staff and has an annual budget of around £1.8 billion.

## Why Is This a Separate Body?

The Environment Agency exists as an arm's-length body — separate from DEFRA, its sponsoring department — to insulate environmental regulation from ministerial interference. The theory: a minister shouldn't be able to overrule a pollution prosecution because the polluter is politically connected. Environmental enforcement should be independent of political pressure.

The theory is reasonable. The practice has produced an institution that operates in a sealed bubble. The independence that prevents a minister from blocking a prosecution also prevents a minister from saying "your spring chamber requirement is legitimate, but when combined with the local authority veto and the planning inspectorate timeline, the aggregate cost is tens of millions — please weigh that." Nobody can say that to the EA because nobody has the authority. The independence is symmetrical: it blocks bad interference and good coordination equally.

The question Stagnacarta asks of every arm's-length body is the same: does the separation serve the public, or does it primarily serve the institution's own comfort? For the Environment Agency, the answer is probably "both, and nobody has ever been forced to choose."

## The Divergence

The EA's mandate is environmental protection. Within that mandate, it operates rationally and often effectively. The spring chamber requirement at HS2 Wendover is a reasonable engineering precaution — groundwater monitoring near a major tunnel is standard practice.

The problem is not that the EA's individual requirements are unreasonable. The problem is that the EA has no mandate — and no incentive — to weigh its requirements against the aggregate cost of compliance when combined with every other regulatory body's requirements.

Each requirement it imposes is individually defensible. The aggregate weight of those requirements, combined with every other body's requirements, is never measured — because measuring it is nobody's job.

Meanwhile, the EA's own enforcement has collapsed — prosecutions at 6% of previous levels. The Ofwat intersection is another blind spot: the EA regulates water quality while Ofwat regulates water companies, DEFRA owns water policy, and nobody owns the intersection where fibre optic leak detection technology could save 3 billion litres of treated water per day. Belgium is deploying it operationally while the UK pilots.

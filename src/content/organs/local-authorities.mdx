## What They Do

Local authorities deliver the vast majority of public services people actually encounter: social care, children's services, roads, waste collection, planning, environmental health, trading standards, libraries, parks. They are funded through council tax, business rates, and central government grants.

## Why Are These Separate From Central Government?

Local authorities exist because some decisions should be made locally — planning, waste collection, parks, local roads. The principle is democratic subsidiarity: decisions should be taken as close as possible to the people they affect.

The problem is that this principle has been weaponised in both directions. Central government uses local authorities to deliver nationally mandated services (SEND, social care) while refusing to fund them adequately — outsourcing the blame for failure. Local authorities use their democratic mandate to block nationally significant infrastructure (HS2 farm tracks, tram lines, housing) on behalf of local residents who bear none of the systemic cost.

The result is the worst of both worlds: local authorities are too weak to resist central mandates and too strong to be overridden on national projects. They have the accountability of local government with the fiscal autonomy of a branch office.

The question is not whether local government should exist — it obviously should. The question is whether the current division of powers, funding, and accountability between central and local government serves anyone except the politicians at both levels who benefit from being able to blame each other.

## The Divergence

In 2025-26, the average local authority spends 47% of its budget on adult social care and children's services — up 57% from 2015-16. These are statutory obligations: councils must provide them regardless of whether they can afford to. Everything else gets crushed.

Councils go bankrupt (Northamptonshire, Thurrock, Slough, Birmingham) not from extravagance but from the mathematical impossibility of funding open-ended central mandates from a fixed local tax base.

Voters see rising council tax and deteriorating local services. They change the party in control. The new administration faces identical statutory constraints. Local elections become performative — the incoming party cannot change the spending priorities because central government has already determined them.

Local authorities routinely deny SEND provision they know they'll lose on appeal, because the delay saves money. Buckinghamshire Council blocked a farm track upgrade that their own planning officers recommended approving, accountable to Dobbins Lane residents rather than national taxpayers bearing the delay costs.

## What It Does

The Department for Education is responsible for children's services and education in England, including early years, schools, higher and further education, apprenticeships, and wider skills.

## Why Does It Delegate So Much?

The DfE is a ministerial department — the Secretary of State is directly accountable to Parliament. In theory this means democratic oversight of education policy. In practice the DfE has created a constellation of arm's-length bodies (Ofsted, QAA, Office for Students, ESFA) that each own a piece of the system without any of them owning the outcome.

The DfE sets the policy. Ofsted inspects schools against it. The QAA assesses university processes (not standards). The OfS regulates the higher education market. The ESFA handles funding. Local authorities deliver SEND. The DfE itself does remarkably little delivery — it designs frameworks and delegates implementation to bodies it then claims not to control.

This is not arm's-length independence for a good reason (like keeping environmental enforcement away from political pressure). This is a ministerial department that has structurally insulated itself from accountability for the outcomes of its own policies. When SEND fails, the DfE points to local authorities. When university standards collapse, it points to the QAA. When schools fail, it points to Ofsted. The architecture is designed to ensure that the minister is never the last person standing.

## The Divergence

The DfE optimises for measurable throughput — participation rates, attainment statistics, numbers of EHCPs issued — rather than the outcomes those numbers are supposed to represent.

More students go to university (metric achieved). Graduate literacy and numeracy rank in the bottom third of the OECD (outcome failed). More EHCPs are issued (metric achieved). Parents spend years fighting for provision that never materialises (outcome failed).

The DfE sets the statutory framework that entitles children with special educational needs to Education, Health and Care Plans. It funds local authorities to deliver these plans — but at a level that makes compliance mathematically impossible for many councils. Parents win 96% of tribunal appeals, demonstrating systematic non-compliance. The DfE sets the obligation; local authorities bear the cost; tribunals enforce the gap. Nobody owns the aggregate failure.

The DfE also created and oversees the Quality Assurance Agency, which explicitly does not assess academic standards — only whether processes exist to describe what will be taught. The Robbins Report mentioned standards 72 times and processes twice. The QAA inverted that ratio.

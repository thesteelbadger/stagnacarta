## The Problem

The Special Educational Needs and Disabilities system in England is in crisis. Parents of children with additional needs face a Kafkaesque journey: NHS assessment delays of years, local authorities that routinely refuse Education, Health and Care Plans (EHCPs) they are statutorily required to provide, and a tribunal system where parents win 96% of appeals — demonstrating systematic non-compliance by councils, not marginal cases.

Local authorities cannot afford the statutory obligations central government has placed on them. The result is a system where the law says your child is entitled to support, the council says it can't afford to provide it, and you spend months or years fighting through tribunals to get what should have been automatic.

## The Scale

New data from the DWP Family Resources Survey (March 2026) reveals the scale:

12% of children — approximately 1.7 million — are now reported by parents as having a long-term illness, disability or impairment. This has nearly doubled from 7% in 2015. Almost two-thirds cite "social or behavioural" impairment as the primary issue.

The number of children with behavioural disorders claiming Disability Living Allowance has almost quadrupled to 276,000 since before the pandemic — including 10,000 under-fives and 14 babies under one year old.

Overall child health/disability benefit spending is forecast to rise from £5.3 billion to £8.3 billion by the start of the next decade.

## The Measurement Question

How much of the doubling is genuine increased prevalence (better diagnosis of autism and ADHD), how much is diagnostic expansion (lowered thresholds), how much is changed reporting behaviour (reduced stigma), and how much is benefits system incentives? Nobody can tell you, because the measurement instrument — what counts as "disabled" — has changed, and the government doesn't even have a shared definition of "behavioural disorders."

A £5.3 billion spending line growing at double digits with no shared understanding of what's driving it. The system counts diagnoses without being able to distinguish genuine need from system-gaming, making reform politically impossible without being accused of attacking disabled children.

## The Multi-Organ Failure

The DfE sets the policy. Local authorities fund it. The NHS assesses it. Tribunals enforce it. Nobody owns the gap between what the law requires and what the system can deliver.

Parents win 96% of tribunal appeals. That number alone tells you the system is not working — it means local authorities are routinely denying support they know they'll lose on appeal, because the delay saves money even if the outcome doesn't change. The process is the punishment.

## What Needs to Happen

This case study requires substantial additional research. The tribunal data, local authority funding gaps, NHS assessment delays, and the interaction between the SEND system and the broader statutory straitjacket facing councils all need documenting with precision.

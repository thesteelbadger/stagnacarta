## Why It Worked

The UK's COVID vaccine procurement (2020–21) was the fastest and most effective in the world. The Vaccines Taskforce, led by Kate Bingham, secured early contracts with multiple manufacturers before efficacy was proven — a venture-capital approach to portfolio risk that contradicted normal civil service procurement doctrine.

Three conditions met simultaneously:

**Technically expert leadership.** Bingham was a life sciences investor, not a civil servant. She understood how biotech procurement and risk worked.

**Genuine decision-making authority.** The Taskforce operated outside normal Cabinet Office procurement processes, reporting directly to ministers.

**Protection from the institutional immune system.** The emergency context meant normal approval processes were suspended.

Remove any one of these and you revert to standard procurement — which would have produced the same outcome as the EU: later, slower, more cautious.

## The EU Contrast

The European Commission ran vaccine procurement through normal multilateral processes, optimised for price negotiation and member state equity rather than speed. The result was months of delay and significant excess mortality. The institutional process produced the institutionally correct output — and the wrong outcome.

## The Question It Raises

This is the clearest modern UK example of deliberately bypassing the institutional immune system by creating a parallel structure with different accountability rules and a technically expert leader. It worked. The question is whether the conditions that made it work — genuine emergency, political will to override normal processes, the right person with the right authority — can be created deliberately rather than stumbled into.

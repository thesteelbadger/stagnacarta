## The Facts

The Post Office's Horizon IT system (built by Fujitsu/ICL) had known bugs creating false accounting discrepancies. Fujitsu knew and told the Post Office board. The Post Office chose to prosecute sub-postmasters rather than investigate the system. Over 900 were prosecuted between 1999 and 2015. Some were imprisoned. Some lost their homes. Some died by suicide.

The Post Office used private prosecution powers — it could charge, prosecute and convict without CPS review.

## The Cultural Failure

Post Office management looked at sub-postmasters — often small business people, frequently from immigrant communities, running corner shops and village post offices — and saw potential thieves, not colleagues. The institution's default assumption was human dishonesty, not system error. This persisted for over a decade despite mounting evidence.

## The Structural Problem

Private prosecution powers meant no CPS oversight. A commercial organisation wielded criminal law against people who had no resources to fight back. The burden of proof was effectively reversed for people without social capital.

## What Broke the Story

Not the formal institutions — not the CPS, not the CCRC, not the sponsoring department. It broke because ITV made a drama. Decades of campaigning by Alan Bates and others created the conditions, but the formal oversight mechanisms failed completely.

## The Fujitsu Question

Fujitsu will never pay meaningful damages because their liability is contractual — faulty software is a commercial dispute. The Post Office's liability is criminal — knowingly prosecuting innocent people with evidence they had reason to doubt. Different categories of wrong. The public conflates them because "big tech company" is a more satisfying villain than "middle managers at a government-owned corporation."

It was the board and the managers who decided sub-postmasters were thieves. They need accountability.

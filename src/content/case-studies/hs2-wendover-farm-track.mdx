## The Facts

HS2's Wendover Green Tunnel requires a spring chamber — an underground concrete box with groundwater sensors, mandated by the Environment Agency. No spring chamber, no tunnel. To build and maintain it, engineers needed to upgrade an existing farm track off Dobbins Lane: two strips of permeable gravelly-concrete under each wheel rut. The spring chamber itself is buried and invisible.

## The Failure

Buckinghamshire Council refused planning permission, claiming the track would harm the "sensitive and protected Chilterns National Landscape" — overruling their own planning officers who recommended approval.

The actual "construction traffic" that spooked them: a single truck per day during a 12-week construction period, outside peak hours.

The council meeting was described as "absolutely maddening." HS2 came with a robust, minimal plan already adjusted based on local feedback, with full support from officers. The councillors rejected it anyway.

## The Resolution

Revised application using temporary access via Kings Farm instead. Same outcome, different route, months of delay, tens of millions in additional cost. Wendover earthworks now expected to extend into 2029.

## Why This Matters

This is a clean case study in the accountability gap. The Environment Agency set a non-negotiable requirement. HS2 designed the minimum possible intervention to meet it. Local councillors — accountable only to residents on Dobbins Lane, not to taxpayers nationally — blocked it on vague landscape grounds.

No single actor was "wrong" within their own frame of reference. The systemic cost (tens of millions, months of delay on a project already haemorrhaging money) falls on nobody in particular. Classic siloed accountability — nobody owns the aggregate consequence.

## The Comparative Question

France builds high-speed rail at roughly £30m per mile. HS2 costs £450m per mile. The Wendover farm track is a component of that differential — not the cause, but a perfect illustration of how regulatory accretion, local veto powers, and institutional risk aversion compound to make British infrastructure extraordinarily expensive.

## This Is Not Declinism

Britain has been declaring itself in decline since the 1880s. Germans were going to bury us. Then the Japanese. Then the Americans. Then the Europeans. Robert Tombs has documented at least four distinct waves of this. David Edgerton has spent a career demolishing it. They're largely right. Britain was not in permanent decline.

Something different happened after 2008. Sixteen years of flat real wages has never occurred in modern British history. Productivity flatlined. Infrastructure decayed. Institutional capacity hollowed out. This is not the latest chapter of a very old British habit. It is historically specific, measurable, and genuinely unprecedented.

Stagnacarta's thesis: the Blair/Brown era (1997–2010) built a genuine institutional architecture — minimum wage, devolution, Bank of England independence, NHS rebuild, Sure Start, the beginnings of digital government. That architecture proved catastrophically fragile. A decade of austerity and political indifference hollowed it out completely.

The structure is a three-act story. What was built. What broke and why. What the wreckage means for the problems coming down the track.

## Always Ask "Compared to What?"

Every mechanism documented on this site is tested against international comparators. If France has metric capture and Germany has accountability diffusion and the US has regulatory accretion, we say so honestly. Where Britain is genuinely distinctive, we show the evidence. Where it's not, we say that too, and ask what the comparator did differently.

This is almost never done in UK policy debate. The NHS is always compared to the American insurance system, never to the Dutch or Australian models. Planning is compared to Texas, never to the Netherlands. Infrastructure costs are compared to France — but nobody asks why France builds cheaper through a system with more regulation, not less.

The comparative discipline is not decoration. It is the analytical method that stops this site becoming another echo chamber telling its own tribe what it wants to hear.

## Anger as Precision, Not Volume

The editorial voice on this site demonstrates anger through relentlessness of documentation, not through adjectives. The reader should arrive at outrage independently through accumulating evidence. Then, and only then, one sentence of editorial voice lands like a hammer.

The wrong model: sustained shouting that validates people who already agree. The right model: fury compressed into precision. Controlled detonation, not sustained shouting.

## Never Reject a Statistic Without Offering Better

When this site questions official statistics, it always offers alternative data sources that tell a different story. Metric capture applied to measurement systems is a specific analytical tool, not grounds for general scepticism.

If you can point to insurance claims data, A&E assault presentations, or victim survey breakdowns by offence type — that's analysis. If you're just saying "it doesn't feel right" — that's not analysis. That's Farage.

Sometimes a metric is wrong and there is no replacement. That's difficult. We say so when it happens.

## Reforms: Tradeoff Register, Not Proposals

This site does not propose its own reforms. It takes other people's proposed reforms and stress-tests them honestly — documenting what each would actually mean, who would lose, why it hasn't happened despite being obviously sensible, and what would break.

Everyone proposes reforms. Nobody does the impact assessment. Every proposed change to a production system gets assessed for impact, risk, dependencies, and rollback plan. Nobody does this for policy proposals. This is the section we want cited by select committees.

## The Division of Labour

This site is built with extensive AI assistance. This is disclosed transparently because the site's credibility depends on readers knowing what was researched by a machine and what was argued by a human.

The reference layer is factual, sourced, AI-assisted, and openly labelled. It links to existing resources — Wikipedia, the Institute for Government, GOV.UK — rather than duplicating them.

The editorial layer is written by Henry, in his voice, with his name on it. The analysis, the connections, the anger. This is the part that cannot be automated.

The test: if a knowledgeable reader thinks "this person has thought about these problems differently from how I think about them" — the project has succeeded. If they think "well-prompted AI output" — it hasn't.

## External Critique

Finished content is periodically fed to other AI systems with instructions to argue against the thesis as strongly as possible. Claude arrives in every conversation primed with this project's framework and vocabulary. The echo chamber risk is real. External critique from systems with different training data and different tendencies is the best antidote to compounding bias.
